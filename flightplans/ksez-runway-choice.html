KSEZ Sedona
Why Runway 3 Wins — Even With a Tailwind

Research report for N269PG operations · May 2026 · mrnonce.com

The Short Answer

At Sedona, conventional "land into the wind" wisdom breaks down because the airport has stacked multiple asymmetric hazards that make Runway 21 more dangerous than a tailwind landing on Runway 3 — up to about 10 knots.

The FAA agrees. The official Chart Supplement entry for KSEZ reads:

RWY 03 CALM WIND RWY FOR LNDG; RWY 21 FOR TKOFF.

This is not a local quirk or pilot folklore. It is codified in federal aeronautical data, and for good reason. The rest of this report explains exactly why.

Sedona Airport sits on top of a 500-foot mesa. There is no soft runway overrun, no EMAS, no second chance. Both ends of Runway 3/21 end at cliff edges that drop 40–45 degrees to the valley floor. The consequences of a runway overrun in either direction are severe. This is the fundamental constraint that shapes every decision made at KSEZ.

At high-elevation airports with short runways and cliff edges at the end, this is not a small consideration. It is the primary factor.

Five Reasons Runway 3 Is Preferred

Reason 1

🏔 The Runway Slopes Uphill — Physics Helps You Stop

FAA runway data confirms: Runway 03 threshold sits at 4,736 ft; Runway 21 threshold sits at 4,830 ft. The 94-foot elevation difference over 5,132 feet gives a 1.83% uphill slope when landing on Runway 03.

Landing uphill is one of the most effective natural braking aids available. On a 1.83% grade, aircraft weight assists deceleration rather than fighting it. Landing downhill (Runway 21) has the opposite effect — weight adds to the kinetic energy that must be dissipated by brakes and rolling friction alone, with a cliff waiting at the end.

The NTSB determined in the 2011 JetSuite accident that Runway 21 "features a 1.9 percent downward sloping gradient" as a direct contributing factor in the aircraft's inability to stop. The aircraft's calculated landing distance exceeded the runway length by over 500 feet.

Reason 2

💨 Mesa-Edge Downdrafts on the Runway 21 Approach

When winds come from the south or southwest — which is frequent — air flowing over the mesa creates severe mechanical turbulence and downdrafts at the approach end of Runway 21. The effect is described in the official airport remarks:

ARPT ON 500 FT MESA; WS, TURB, UP & DOWN DRAFTS NEAR MESA EDGE.

BoldMethod, which flew a Cirrus SR22T into Sedona specifically to document this, reported requiring a 10-knot speed increase on a 25-knot wind day to compensate for anticipated altitude loss near the threshold. The mechanism: wind flowing over the edge of a 500-foot vertical drop creates a rotor that pushes aircraft down just as they cross the edge and flare. An aircraft that is already high and fast — which is the natural error response to sink fear — then has excess speed, floats, and uses up precious runway.

On the Runway 03 approach (from the southwest), you're approaching over the lower terrain of the valley, not across a cliff edge. The turbulence profile is significantly better.

Reason 3

📡 Only One Instrument Approach — And It's to Runway 03

The terrain to the northwest rises sharply, making any instrument approach to Runway 21 impossible. The sole published instrument approach at KSEZ is the RNAV (GPS) RWY 03. This is not accidental — it reflects the only safe approach corridor.

The PAPI glidepath angles tell the same story:

The steeper 3.5° glidepath for Runway 21 is necessary to clear terrain and obstacles on the northeast side. A steeper approach means a faster descent rate, which means more float tendency and more tendency to land long — the exact failure mode that overruns Runway 21.

Reason 4

🚀 Go-Around Geometry Favors Runway 03

Every approach should be flown with a go-around in mind. The go-around terrain picture at KSEZ is different for each runway — and this is one factor where Runway 21 actually holds an advantage over Runway 03.

  • Go-around from Runway 21: climbing southwest on ~212° heading, straight into the valley. Lower terrain ahead. Cleanest escape route at KSEZ.
  • Go-around from Runway 03: landing heading is ~032° (northeast). Straight-out puts you initially toward the red rock formations. Left traffic pattern requires a left turn to the NW/W to reach lower terrain — manageable, but not a clean straight-ahead picture.

On go-around geometry alone, Runway 21 wins. The BYTER ONE Obstacle Departure Procedure (the only published ODP at KSEZ) reflects the same geometry — climb southwest on departure, which is the same direction as a Runway 21 go-around. Note however: this factor is outweighed by the other four reasons Runway 03 is preferred for landing — specifically the downslope, the approach-end downdraft, the overrun asymmetry, and the accident record. The go-around situation from Runway 21 is cleaner, but you're far less likely to need a go-around on Runway 03 in the first place because the approach is more stable and the threshold environment is less turbulent.

Reason 5

⚡ The Overrun Consequence Is Not Symmetric

Both ends of the runway have cliffs. But they are not equal.

  • Runway 03 departure end (northeast): cliff edge, terrain drops to Oak Creek Canyon. Overrun area exists. Still severe.
  • Runway 21 departure end (southwest): according to NTSB findings in the 2011 accident, the terrain past the departure end of Runway 21 drops at 40–45 degrees for approximately 800 feet to the valley floor. The overrun area is only 123 feet.

The 2011 JetSuite Phenom 100 experienced this firsthand. The crew survived, but the aircraft cartwheeled down the embankment. EMAS (Engineered Material Arresting System) has been discussed for KSEZ but not installed as of this writing. There is almost no margin for error landing Runway 21.

The 10-Knot Tailwind Rule — Why It Makes Physical Sense

Pilots at KSEZ commonly accept up to 10 knots of tailwind to land on Runway 03. This surprises students who are taught "never land with a tailwind." Here's why the math at KSEZ justifies it.

RWY 03 with 10kt Tailwind

  • Uphill slope assists braking throughout rollout
  • Tailwind increases groundspeed — but you're landing on the uphill end with more stopping force
  • Approach turbulence is low — no cliff-edge rotor to surprise you
  • Stable approach geometry (3.00° PAPI)
  • Left pattern escape NW/W to valley if go-around needed; less clean than RWY 21 straight-out
  • NTSB record: far fewer overruns on RWY 03

RWY 21 with 10kt Headwind

  • Downhill slope actively extends stopping distance
  • Headwind reduces groundspeed, but downslope removes that advantage
  • Approach rotor/downdraft zone is active with SW winds — exactly when RWY 21 would be "preferred"
  • Steeper 3.50° PAPI means faster-than-normal descent rate
  • Only 123 ft overrun, then cliff
  • Accounts for multiple overrun accidents
⚠ The 10-knot figure is a rule of thumb, not an FAA limit The POH for your SR22T lists a maximum demonstrated crosswind of 21 knots and a recommended tailwind limit of 10 knots. At high density altitudes, landing speeds are higher in TAS — this matters. A 10-knot tailwind in cool morning conditions is different from 10 knots at 8,500 ft DA on a hot afternoon. Calculate your specific landing distance for conditions, not just the wind number.

The Accident Record — What Has Actually Happened

JetSuite Phenom 100 — May 25, 2011 — Runway 21 Overrun

This is the definitive KSEZ case study. A Citation Mustang-class jet overran Runway 21, went down the embankment, and was destroyed. Pilots and passengers survived.

"Upon turning final, he thought the airplane was high and reduced power to idle. As the airplane neared the approach end, it felt like it was pushed up by a wind shift to a tailwind or an updraft and the airplane touched down firmly near the runway numbers... the airplane subsequently exited the departure end and traveled down an embankment."
— NTSB Accident Brief WPR11FA236
  • A witness on CTAF tried to warn the crew to use Runway 03 — they landed 21 anyway
  • Runway 21 confirmed to have 1.9% downward slope (NTSB finding)
  • Aircraft touched down at 128 KIAS vs. recommended 101 KIAS
  • Required landing distance exceeded runway length by over 500 feet
  • Overrun area past Runway 21: 123 feet. Beyond that: 800-foot cliff at 40–45 degrees
  • The updraft/wind shift at the threshold is a documented mesa-edge effect

The JetSuite accident is the most-cited example, but it is not unique. Sedona has seen dozens of runway overruns over the decades, the vast majority on Runway 21. The pattern is consistent: pilots underestimate the downslope, arrive fast due to mesa-edge induced float, and run out of runway with nowhere safe to go.

📊 The NTSB Pattern at KSEZ NTSB records for KSEZ skew heavily toward Runway 21 overruns and excursions. The common thread: pilots who chose Runway 21 for a headwind landing and were surprised by the runway's downslope behavior combined with turbulence/float from the mesa edge. Runway 03 overruns are rare at KSEZ — the uphill slope generally provides enough buffer.

When IS Runway 21 the Right Call for Landing?

Wind above ~10 knots from the northeast — use Runway 21 When winds are strongly out of the northeast (from the Runway 03 direction), the calculus changes. Strong NE winds create their own downdraft hazard on the Runway 03 approach end — the same mesa-edge rotor effect, but now on the 03 side. The rule of thumb inverts: beyond about 10 knots of headwind on Runway 03, you should consider Runway 21 despite the downslope.

The correct framework isn't "always land Runway 03" — it's recognizing that KSEZ has two distinct dangerous conditions:

  • Strong SW winds → downdraft zone activates on Runway 21 approach end → land Runway 03
  • Strong NE winds → downdraft zone activates on Runway 03 approach end → consider Runway 21, carefully
  • Light or calm winds → land Runway 03 (uphill, no rotor hazard, FAA preference)
  • Moderate tailwind (<10kt) from SW → land Runway 03 anyway (slope benefit outweighs tailwind penalty)

The key operational skill at KSEZ is recognizing which hazard configuration is active on that day, not blindly defaulting to either runway.

💡 The visual illusion trap — know it before you arrive The mesa cliff edge past Runway 03 creates a visual illusion during the flare. The ground drops away sharply beyond the threshold, which can make you think you're higher than you are. Do not let this prompt you to descend aggressively. Trust the PAPI. Fly a stable, on-glidepath approach and execute a normal flare — the runway is right where it should be.
⚠ Go-around discipline On a high-DA day at KSEZ, if you're not stabilized by 500 ft AGL on short final — go around immediately. A go-around from Runway 03 requires a left turn to the NW/W to stay clear of terrain; straight-out on 032° goes toward the red rocks. The go-around decision is made on base, not on final — left pattern puts you well-positioned for a NW escape if needed.
